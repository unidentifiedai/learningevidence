INTRODUCTION

Tagakaulo Muslim Tribe

The Tagakaulo, or Tagakaolo, are people who traditionally lived near water sources, such as rivers or streams, and have a population of over 100,000. They were originally found on the west coast of the Davao Gulf and the southern side of Mount Apo. Today, most Tagakaulo natives reside in various locations, including the coasts, riverbanks, valleys, mountains, and plains of Davao del Sur, as well as in Sarangani Province, particularly along the Malalag, Lais, and Talaguton Rivers, and in areas such as Santa Maria, Malita in Davao Occidental, and Malungon. They referred to themselves as Kalagan and were known as Tagakaulo only by outsiders.

ART STYLE!

The Tagakaulo tribe of the Philippines showcases their unique art style through intricate craftsmanship and the use of natural materials, particularly in their traditional attire and accessories. One striking example is the Dagom Malimot, a blouse worn by Tagakaulo women, typically made in shades of blue or black and adorned with exquisite embroidery and mother-of-pearl shell disks.

This intricate detailing reflects the tribe's deep connection to nature and their artistic ingenuity. Complementing this garment, women incorporate horsehair into their head accessories, creating elaborate and distinctive adornments that symbolize both beauty and cultural identity. These artistic elements demonstrate the Tagakaulo's creativity and their reverence for natural materials, showcasing a harmonious blend of function, tradition, and artistic expression.

Summarization

The Tagakaulo tribe, with over 100,000 members, originally inhabited areas around the Davao Gulf and Mount Apo in Mindanao but now predominantly reside in Davao del Sur and Sarangani. They speak a language related to Mandaya and Kamayo and traditionally practiced animism. Their livelihoods center on farming, fishing, and labor, and they are known for their rich warrior traditions, which included using handmade weapons and later adopting firearms. Their cultural heritage is reflected in their traditional attire, such as horsehair headpieces and the Dagom Malimot blouse, a garment adorned with mother-of-pearl shell disks that embodies their artistic expression.

Historically, the Tagakaulo were perceived as rebellious by Spanish and American authorities due to their turbulent and warlike disposition. Renowned for their bravery, they resisted foreign rule through acts of defiance, including their involvement in the assassination of Davao District Governor Edward C. Bolton on June 6, 1906â€”a bold response to American exploitation of their land and labor. Under the leadership of the warrior Paugok, the tribe waged a successful war against the Bagobos. They also practiced brutal traditions during raids, such as cutting victims into pieces and attaching tufts of hair from the slain to their shields, emphasizing their fierce reputation. These acts of resistance and warrior practices underscore the Tagakaulo's enduring commitment to safeguarding their land, culture, and autonomy.
